The Word You Kept


The door was pine, painted a pale brown. Behind it was a grate, a steel lock. Yue Ju knew this not because he could see the grate or the lock; he didn’t have X-ray vision. It was because this was his door. It was the door to his old house.


“Ju.”


What he didn’t understand was why it was here.


“Ju!” Ma’s chopsticks clattered on top of her bowl. Yue Ju snapped his eyes back, found his mother glowering at him. Then, in that abrupt way she had, she sighed, covered her face with a hand. “You ah, when I talk to you, can you listen?”


He looked down at his own bowl. He’d shoved the rice all to one side, put the vegetables and the pork on the other. He didn’t like it when they got mixed up. When he glanced up again, Ma had put her hand down, was looking at him. Ma didn’t like the way she said he was always playing with his food, so he said before she could speak again, “I was listening.”


She raised an eyebrow. “Ah? What did I say?”


Brain, work fast. Result: blank.


Another sigh. But Ma picked up her chopsticks again, so it didn’t look like she was going to scold him. Ma was nicer to him before she had to fly off again. Aunty had told him once it was because Ma felt guilty, leaving him on his own in the house all the time. Yue Ju didn’t see why she should. He was used to being on his own in the house. He liked being on his own in the house. It was quiet. He could read his Doraemon and there would be no sound at all, except for the ones he made. He like d that there was no sound.


Ma was talking again. “You’ll be okay taking the bus, right?”


“Ah.”


“It would be better if Aunty could send you to school like last time.” Another sigh. Ma also sighed a lot before she had to fly off again. Aunty had told him it was because Ma’s job was tiring. Yue Ju didn’t know why she didn’t just quit and do something else, then. But the one time he suggested it Ma got this strange, angry look on her face and told him never to ask her that again. So he didn’t.


Yue Ju didn’t like saying much anyway. Quiet was better.


He dug his chopsticks into his rice, snuck another glance at the door. Under the stark lights of the kitchen, the brown paint on it looked almost white. What was it doing here? He was pretty sure doors just didn’t uproot themselves and move to new homes with their owners because they didn’t want to be left behind.


And then it rattled. Not the door. That stayed still. The steel lock. He could hear it. He knew what it sounded like, because once, he didn’t remember when, he’d stood outside that door and that grate and listened to the person beside him rattle the lock, rattle it and rattle it. He didn’t know who this person was.


And then it banged. Not the door. That stayed still. The black grate. He could hear it. He knew what it sounded like, because when the lock wouldn’t just break open, locks didn’t do that, the person beside him had gripped the grate and tried to shake it. Bang, bang, bang.


Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang, bang.


“Ju!”


He jumped. Ma was glaring at him again. “You ah,” she said. “You never listen.”


Yue Ju glanced at the door that had appeared on the wall of his new kitchen, in his new house, and said, “Sorry.”


The door. It rattled and it banged all night. Yue Ju went to his room. He locked his bedroom door. He put on his headphones, turned on his laptop and played Daughtry’s new album at maximum volume. His ears felt like they were being drummed to pieces. He sat on his new bed and read Doraemon Volume 9 again.


He fell asleep like that, the album on repeat.


As he was sleeping, he turned over and the laptop at the edge of the bed slid, slid over and hit the floor. His headphones came unplugged. Daughtry blasted loudly into the night, but Yue Ju’s ears weren’t drumming anymore. He bolted upright. Looked around wildly for a moment, then reached down and slammed his laptop shut. The sound cut off.


He covered his face with his hands. Had Ma heard? She would be really mad, because the neighbours would be mad, and she had to fly off early tomorrow.


But Ma didn’t come. There was no slamming door, no voice yelling at Ju, asking him what the hell he was doing at this time of night.


Slowly, Ju removed his hands.


There were two doors.


His bedroom door, and beside it, the pale brown one that he knew had a grate and a lock behind it. He stared at it.


As he stared at it, it began to rattle. Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang, rattle, bang, rattle, bang, bang.


“Ju,” Ma said when he was taking the rest of his cereal to dump into the sink. His eyes felt like stone and his stomach felt like acid. He wanted to go back to sleep. He wanted to sleep in general. “The incense sticks are under the sink. Get them, please.”


He didn’t mean to sound sullen. He was just tired. “Why are they under the sink? Ma, you’re supposed to put them under the altar.”


“Ah,” Ma said, voice steel. “Sorry, ah. Your mother is not home enough, so she doesn’t even know where anything goes.”


He didn’t know why it mattered so much to Ma, that she was never home. It didn’t matter to him. He’d never even said anything about it. She was still talking, saying the same things she always said, as he got the incense sticks, put them where they were supposed to be, then took two from the pack, lit them and holding them, stood in front of the altar and said a few words in his head to Ba and Kor. Then he put the sticks in the pot, and by the time he was done, Ma had stopped talking.


Kor had taught him to do that, he remembered. He didn’t remember much about Kor, because he had been seven when Ba and Kor died. But little things, like how not to add fuel to the fire when Ma and Ba were fighting again, because trying to get them to make up and not be angry anymore was to get in their way. Like how, one night, very late, when he was already in his pyjamas, Kor was standing just inside their door, the door at the old house and saying – something.


Like the look on Kor’s face. His eyes wide. His voice strained. Saying – something.


Yue Ju went to put on his shoes. He should go and wait outside, in case the bus came. He’d never taken the bus before, but he’d asked his friends and they said that where he lived, the bus would be early. Maybe Hong An would be on the bus already, and they could sit together.


He put his shoes on in the hallway. New Bata shoes, white and clean. Ma always took him to buy new shoes, new clothes, a new school bag, at the start of every year. She always made sure to be home for a whole day, and they went to Metro Kajang and ate longan with shaved ice after.


“Ju,” Ma said. She was standing behind him, just under the archway between the hall and the kitchen. “Be good, okay?”


Yue Ju strapped on his left shoe, then as he was reaching for his bag, sat next to the shoe rack, he looked up. There were two doors. The front door, and the pale brown one with the grate and the lock.


He stood up. Rattle, went the lock. Bang, went the grate. Rattle, bang. He took one step. Another step. Reached up for the knob on his front door. This front door. The door to his new house. Beside him: rattle, bang. Rattle, bang, bang.


What would happen if he opened it?


He moved his hand.


“You ah,” Ma said. “What are you doing? Cannot see, ah?”


He moved his hand back, put it on the right knob. It was cold in his fingers. “Have a safe flight,” he said to Ma.


When he closed the front door, stood outside in the early morning dark and looked back, there was only one door. Of course there was. He walked down the driveway, past the garden where they hadn’t grown anything yet, or bought any flower pots to put in it, and out of the gate. He locked the gate behind him again with his new set of keys. At the very end of the road, there were lights. White lights cutting through the night and coming towards him.


It had been night time, when Ba and Kor died.


The bus was here. Hong An was on it already. They sat together and Hong An asked him questions, but Yue Ju was so tired from staying awake all night that he fell asleep. He fell asleep in class too, and when Teacher slammed her book down on his table, it sounded a lot like ‘bang’.


No one laughed. Teacher was very strict. She had a nose and eyebrows and lips all like the Staedtler 2B pencils Ma bought him boxes of for his exams. Very straight and very thin and very sharp.


“Lim Yue Ju,” she said. “Is my class so boring that you must sleep in it?”


Teacher spoke English in the same way. Straight, thin and sharp.


Yue Ju’s English was very bad. All the words were like rocks in his head, and he couldn’t really fit rocks together, because they weren’t like jigsaw puzzle pieces, which had clear shapes so you could fit them into a bigger picture. English words just…were. “Um,” he said. “No. Sorry.”


His ears were turning red. He could feel how warm they were. He was warm all over. Morning was gone and afternoon was coming, and in his long prefect sleeves with the collar buttoned up and the tie choking at his throat, it was like sitting in an oven. There was no escape.


“So you weren’t sleeping?”


“No.” He shouldn’t have lied.


Teacher’s face was turning sharper. “Stand up,” she said. “Read the next page.”


His chair scraped on the concrete floor. He lifted his book.


“Then, at last, after a fortnight of horrible capteeveet – ”


“Captivity.”


“Captivity,” he repeated. “During which I was filled with pity, entoo – ”


“Enthusiasm.”


What was the point of reading this difficult book anyway? Teacher said that they were class 2-A, and that the version in the syllabus was too easy, and they were going to read the original to challenge themselves. But Yue Ju didn’t understand what the point was. The story was the same. Only the words were different. Did words matter?


“Enthusiasm, despair and horror by turns, he believed me when I said, I said – ”


Kor, standing just inside their old front door, still in his pyjamas, Ba yelling behind him, yelling at them both, and Kor was saying, Kor who was good at English and liked watching all those old medieval English movies on TV that Yue Ju didn’t understand, “I will come back.”


Kor liked to say things all cool and dramatic like that, like he heard people say them in movies. Kor believed that words were magic. That if you said them right, they could become a spell.


Yue Ju didn’t.


Yue Ju liked the quiet.


With the lock rattling and the grate banging, there was no quiet.


He went home. There was only one front door. He cooked lunch for himself. Ma wasn’t on the plane anymore, but she hadn’t texted him. She was probably busy with her clients. He ladled rice for himself, pushing it to one side of the bowl, and then dumped the vegetables and the chicken from the wok into the other side. Then he went into the living room, bent down and dug the remote out of the side of the sofa.


When he straightened, the door was there. Behind the TV. Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang. Rattle, rattle, bang, bang. Bang.


Yue Ju put his bowl down. One step. Another step. It was hard to breathe. He had to do it slowly. Inhale, exhale. Inhale, exhale.


That day, when he and Kor stood outside the door and Kor was rattling and banging away, because they’d lost their keys and it was dark, and Yue Ju was crying, and even though they were going to get into trouble for sneaking out, it was better to be inside than to be out here, cold and scared, Kor had shouted at him. Really loud.


There had been a silence after he shouted. The vibrations of Kor’s voice in the night. The silence had been even louder.


Just as Yue Ju was about to reach up, touch the door’s knob, he heard that silence. He snapped his head to the side.


Found himself looking at the window.


At some point, the sky outside had grown dark. If the window were open, if the air could come in, he knew his nostrils would have been filled with a thick, earthy smell. One drop, two drop. Then a patter. And then as the glass turned opaque with the battering of the rain, he looked back at the door. It was quiet now.


He backed away, sat down on the sofa. He realised that his hands were trembling. When he clasped them together, the skin was cold.


Ma called after dinner. He was eating in his room, the soundtrack to The Amazing Spiderman 2 drumming into his ears through his headphones. He’d pulled his desk away from the wall, into the centre of the room. There were scrape marks on the floor. He would try to clean them up when he cleaned up the rest of the aparment, the drawers he’d opened, the boxes in the storeroom he upended.


His phone was in front of him, but he was so absorbed in watching Spiderman swing from rooftop to rooftop that she called twice before he picked up.


“What is your phone for?” Ma demanded.


Don’t add fuel to the fire.


“Only know how to make me worried.”


Keep quiet.


Ma’s breath crackled over the line. “How was school? The bus okay?”


“Okay.”


“Okay. Goodnight. Be – ”


“Ma,” he said.


He wasn’t wearing his headphones. If he looked up, if he looked up, the door might be there, in the wall opposite him, behind him, to his left or to his right. The rain had stopped. There was only quiet.


The night Ba and Kor died, Ma and Ba had been fighting. He could hear them, even though he was already in bed, the blanket pulled over his head. He was scared, so he opened his bedroom door, walked carefully, quietly. The floor was wet. A water bottle lay at the top of the staircase. Ma and Ba were throwing things again. This time, they’d been throwing the water bottles they kept on their bedside tables. They must have been lying in bed, then Ma or Ba got angry and left the room, then Ma or Ba came after Ma or Ba and threw a water bottle on the floor. They were both downstairs now. Their voices echoed up the stairs.


Kor’s door was already open. He seemed to be listening. When he saw Yue Ju, he rolled his eyes and then waved for him to come inside. Kor shut the door behind them, switched on the light. “Don't step on anything,” he said.


So Yue Ju had to hug the wall as he walked to Kor’s bed. He sat down, stared at the circle Kor had drawn in chalk on the floor. “What’s that?”


The exercise book met with his head. He peeled it off. “Ow.”


“It’s for a spell. Last page.”


He flipped to the back of the book. “It’s in English.”


Kor was crouched on the ground, drawing another circle inside the first. It would have been better to draw the inside circle first, but Kor was dumb like that. He flicked his eyes up at Yue Ju, grinned. “Stupid egg can’t read it?”


“I can,” he said. The easy words. Like ‘a’ and ‘the’ and ‘gate’. He stuck his tongue out at Kor. “Why are you doing an English spell?”


“Because for this spell, English is best.”


“Why?”


Kor shrugged. “Words are like that. How you say them is important. What you say them in is important too.”


He was finished with the circle. Putting one foot then the other inside, he sat down, crossed his legs. Patted the space in front of him. “Come on.”


Yue Ju got in too, gave Kor back his book. “What does it do?”


Kor took his hands. “Say the words with me, and I’ll show you.”


Outside, Ma and Ba were still screaming, but Kor’s voice was low and rhythmic, a drum in Yue Ju’s ears. He listened to it, and the longer and the harder he listened, the further away Ma’s voice and Ba’s voice became.


Until, when Ba slammed the door open, he was almost in another world.


“Ju.” Ma’s voice was sharp, impatient over the phone. “What lah? Spit it out.”


He squeezed his eyes shut.


The quiet wasn’t supposed to remind him of these things. The quiet was just quiet. Safe. In the silence of his house all alone, there were no cars, no shouting, no nothing. Yue Ju liked the quiet.


“Ma,” he said. “When you were packing up Kor’s old bedroom, where did you put his stuff? I couldn’t find the box.”


“That stuff? I threw it away.”


He opened his eyes. “What?”


Ma said in her steel voice, “What, what? We weren’t taking all that junk to the new house. All that stuff to pack and bring over, who do you think had to do that while you were studying? Moving was so hard already – ”


“I didn’t want to move.”


Why had he said that? But it was true. And he was so angry. So angry that his free hand was trembling again. He turned it over, pressed it flat. “I can’t believe you. You just threw Kor’s stuff away. Did you throw away Ba’s too?”


Ma said, “I should have thrown it all out earlier.”


Neither of them said anything else. The phone went dead. Yue Ju looked at his hand on the desk. Put his other hand next to it.


What was he going to do now? He needed the exercise book. He had been seven. His English was bad. He couldn’t remember the spell. The words Kor said.


It was okay. As long as he didn’t look up, the door wouldn’t come back.


It was okay.


Rattle. Bang. Rattle. Bang. Rattle, bang, bang.


He lifted his head. The pale brown door was on the opposite wall. Between the scrapes the legs of his desk had left.


Yue Ju didn’t have any chalk. He drew on the floor with a marker pen. Ma bought it for him when they went to Metro Kajang, said he might need it for projects or stuff. Ma bought a lot of things she thought he needed. Just in case she wasn’t here.


He drew the inner circle first. Then the outer. Then climbed inside, back to the pine door, painted a pale brown. Crossed his legs. The other half of the circle was empty. He didn’t know what to do now.


Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang. Rattle.


He held out his hands.


When Ba had slammed the door open, he hadn’t even looked at the circle. Or at them. His eyes were wild, blank in that way he got when he was really mad. “Get up,” he barked at them. “We’re leaving.”


Ma’s voice was shrill behind him. “You can’t take my sons.”


Ba’s head disappeared round the wall. “They’re my sons.”


Neither he nor Kor moved. Ba came into the room, pulled them up by their arms, dragged them onto the landing. The floor was wet. Yue Ju slipped, nearly fell. But Ba’s hand was an iron clamp on his arm, so he didn’t. He started to cry.


“Look,” said Ma. “Look, you idiot! Look what you’ve done.”


Ba didn’t look. He was pulling them down the stairs. To the door. Ma followed them, saying more things. Everything was too loud. Yue Ju couldn’t hear what they were both yelling over each other. He wanted Ba to let him go. He wanted to sit down, put his hands over his ears. He wanted quiet.


The front door. The wind blew in, cold. It was raining outside. Ba let go of their hands to fumble with the steel lock. Pushed the black grate open.


Behind them, Ma was crying too. Saying, “Ju, Zong, come here. Come to Ma.”


Ma was calling him. He turned to her, took an automatic step forward.


“Ju, Zong” said Ba. “Come.”


He turned back. He didn’t know what was on his face, that made Ba’s mouth harden like that. Ba grabbed Kor by the arm again, but he didn’t reach out to Yue Ju. “Okay, you stay with your mother. See if I care.”


Yue Ju couldn’t even hear himself over himself now. Ma was crying. Ma was calling out to him, that horrible, long whine in her voice. Yue Ju was seven and a big boy now, going to school with a new shirt and new shoes and a new school bag, but he didn’t want to be left here, alone. “Kor,” he said. “You can’t go.”


And Kor had gotten that look in his eyes. The strange look he sometimes had when he was watching his old medieval English movies on TV, or when he was reading old Chinese books that he always hid from Ma and Ba, or when he was writing down pantun in his exercise book, muttering the words to himself.


Kor had said, “Don’t be stupid. There’s no need to be scared. I’ll come back.” And soft, quiet, just for Yue Ju’s ears alone, he’d whispered, “I will come back.”


“Kor,” he whispered into the quiet. The quiet that the rattling, the banging, broke again and again, turned into a staccato of noise. “I’m sorry. I don’t know the words, but please. It’s okay. You don’t have to.”


In the silence after sound, there were vibrations. Rippling, rippling. Sometimes. They were even louder. The vibrations from Kor’s voice in his ear, the last thing he’d ever said to him, settled against Yue Ju’s held out hands.


When Yue Ju was five years old, two years before Ba and Kor died, Kor had snuck them both outside to play sparklers with a classmate across the street. He brought Yue Ju because Yue Ju had heard him opening the door, and threatened to tell on him.


But Kor had lost the keys, and now they were stuck outside their house, and it was cold, the thick, earthy smell of rain in the air, and they were scared. So Kor rattled and banged on the door, and shouted, but neither Ma nor Ba heard them. And then Kor had shouted at him instead.


When they were both calmer, just sitting together on the steps, Kor said to him, “It’s okay, you know. You don’t have to be scared.”


He’d wiped at his eyes, snapped, “I’m not scared. It’s not like – like there are g-ghosts or anything.” If his nose wasn’t blocked up and his voice wasn’t all shaky, it would have been more convincing.


Kor rolled his eyes at him. Leaned back, his hands flat on the steps behind him. Gazed up at the sky. There weren’t any stars, not like in the storybooks. “There are ghosts though.”


Yue Ju squeaked. Covered his mouth with his hands.


Kor laughed at him. “It’s okay,” he said. “It’s really okay! Ghosts won’t hurt you. They’re just here to do stuff, that’s all. Things they’ve promised to do. They aren’t interested in you. When they’re done, they go. That’s it.”


Then he dipped his head, stared at his knees. When he spoke again, his voice was strange. It used to scare Yue Ju, when Kor spoke like that. But he was used to it now. He was seven years old, too old to be scared of anything. “It’s different for bodies. Ghosts can float around, do what they like. But bodies need places. To be in places, to go places, to go back to places. So,” he looked at Ju, face serious. “even though the strongest spells are better linked to concrete things, like bodies and places, it’s better not to do them. Okay?”


Yue Ju frowned. Something had occurred to him. “Is that why you can’t just open the door right now?”


A smile. “Right. Because if I did that, I would be saying, ‘I want to go inside’. Body and place. I would tie myself to this place, and if I ever died and had some kind of unfinished business to do, and had to come back, my body would want to come here.”


“Isn’t that okay?”


Kor shoved him in the shoulder. “You’re stupid, aren’t you? What if what I needed to do wasn’t here?”


Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang. Rattle, bang, rattle, bang, rattle, bang, bang.


Kor had said, I will come back. I will come back.


But this wasn’t home anymore. Kor’s ghost could come here, could follow the lorry and hover in the quiet as Ma and Yue Ju did what they did, lived how they lived.


Rattle, bang. Rattle, BANG. RATTLE BANG RATTLE BANG BANG.


It was cold outside, after the rain. Cold and maybe scary. Kor had been fourteen years old, and now Yue Ju was fourteen years old too, and he knew that at fourteen, you could be scared of anything.


After the accident, Ma hadn’t let him see the bodies. She’d gone to the morgue herself, washed and dressed the bodies for the funeral herself, nailed the coffins shut herself, so that all Yue Ju saw when they lowered Ba and Kor into the ground, was pine.


What did Kor’s body look like, now?


His hands, still held out in front of him, were trembling. Cold. “Kor,” he said. “I’m sorry. I don’t want to know. I’m sorry, but please don’t.” He curled his fingers inwards, into fists, shut his eyes. And shouted, the sound of his voice lost in the noise, “Don’t come back!”


Silence.


Quiet.


Slowly, Yue Ju opened his eyes.


Slowly, he unclenched his fists.


Slowly, very slowly, he turned around.


Creeeeeeaaaaaaaaaaak.
