Where People Don’t Look



Wei Ren woke up on a Monday morning, and his younger sister was gone.


He knew there was something wrong the moment he opened his eyes. The ceiling in his bedroom was dark, the alarm clock was cold beneath his palm, and his brother was shouting outside the door.


After he had put on his school clothes, he went downstairs. The smell of noodles drifted up the stairs. The rustle of newspaper pages turning. His brother’s voice, telling Ma about the mid-term exams that he had today.


Ah, Wei Ren thought. That was what was wrong. Usually, it was Wei Ling who came to wake him up. Why had their brother woken him up instead?


“Good morning, Wei Ren,” said Ma.


Wei Jian was flipping through the newspaper in the bored, self-important way that he did sometimes. “Morning, kid.”


Wei Jian was only two years older than him. Wei Ren glared.


Ma set a plate down in front of him.


Wei Ren picked up his chopsticks, his spoon. It felt a little odd, the spoon. But Wei Ren ignored it. It slipped off the edge of his mind. Like water, like a ghost.


The newspaper-flipping stopped. A chair pushed back.


“What the fuck,” Wei Jian said.


Ma said, iron, “Ah Jian, repeat that.”


Wei Ren looked up at his brother. Wei Jian was standing, his shoulders stiff in his prefect-blue uniform. In the fluorescent glare of the overhead lamp, his brother’s shadow fell across the glass-topped surface of the dining table.


Wei Jian said, “What the fuck happened to your hand?”


Wei Ren looked down at his right hand, gripping his chopsticks. It was fine. He looked at his left hand, gripping his spoon. He put the spoon down. Looked at the hand. Flexed his fingers. One, two, three, four. Ah. Had his hand always been like that? The little finger. Gone.


He wasn’t afraid.


Ma said, “Don’t be rude, Jian. You know your brother’s always been like that.”


Wei Ren went back to eating.


Wei Jian opened his mouth again. Shut it. His shadow receded back across the table. His chair clattered back, slid forward.


Ma dropped them at the gates to the secondary school. The sky was beginning to lighten by this time, but Wei Ren was still cold. Wei Jian ignored him, heading straight for the hall. The prefects were going to gather there, do whatever they did in the electric-lit expanse of the hall before all the other students were allowed in for the morning assembly.


Wei Ren turned left from the gate, heading across the small basketball court towards the Old Building. His classroom was on the third floor.


As he set his bag down by his chair, he brushed his hand across the surface of his desk, sweeping away the eraser dust that the afternoon session Form 1A student had left from the day before. It was his sister’s eraser dust – the family had all laughed when they discovered that Wei Ren and Wei Ling actually shared the same classroom and the same desk.


Ah, Wei Ren thought. His sister. Wei Ling, who woke up early in the morning to go running because she wanted to be a national badminton player.


He looked down at his left hand. The missing finger.


The desk behind his rattled, as a bag was thrown down on it. A shadow fell across Wei Ren’s feet.


“Hey,” said Izzat, in Malay, “Kill me, please.”


Wei Ren only looked at him.


“You won’t do it,” Izzat said. And then he scowled, lifting his head from his arms. His chain necklace, a gift from his mother and that Izzat always wore to school even though he wasn’t allowed to, glinted dully beneath his open collar. “It’s the Maths exam today. And Moral. And Sejarah. I’m going to die anyway.”


Wei Ren pulled out his own chair, sat down on it sideways, resting his arm across the back. He only looked at Izzat.


“You’re not talking,” Izzat noted.


Other students were trickling into the classroom. Chairs scraped back. Bags dropped on the floor. The desks had all been moved apart and into wobbly rows when Wei Ren came in. The class monitor and assistant monitor, he guessed. They were standing by the teacher’s desk, speaking to each other in low voices. The class monitor was fingering the end of her red monitor’s tie.


Wei Jian hadn’t been wearing his prefect’s tie today.


Izzat sighed. Switched to Mandarin. “You’re really chuan, sometimes, you know that. I bet you think you’re going to ace these exams.”


Wei Ren shrugged. He knew that he was arrogant.


“Then what’s with the silent treatment?”


Wei Ren thought of telling Izzat that it wasn’t that he didn’t want to talk, it was that he couldn’t. But why couldn’t he?


He opened his mouth. It felt empty, very empty. He shut it again.


The bell rang for morning assembly.




Wei Ren woke up on a Tuesday morning, and his mother was gone.


He knew there was something wrong the moment he opened his eyes. The air in his bedroom was dark and warm, the alarm clock was cold beneath his palm, and his brother was knocking on the door.


Knocking, knocking, knocking.


Wei Ren changed into his school clothes and went outside.


His brother was sitting at the dining table downstairs. He was eating toast.


“Ma isn’t here,” Wei Jian said. His gaze was cold, hard. It flicked from Wei Ren’s face to his arm to his left hand. “Your fourth finger’s missing.”


Today Wei Ren had his Chinese writing exam. And comprehension. He was worse at languages than at anything else, even languages that were his mother tongue. All the grammar, the expressing of himself. Why did it matter?


He ignored Wei Jian, pulled out a chair, and sat down. Tried to run through the essays that he had memorised the week before. Last night, he hadn’t studied. Just taken a shower and gone to sleep.


He had been very sleepy. And tired of not hearing his own voice.


It was hard to make toast with only one hand and three fingers on the other. Wei Ren didn’t eat it. He couldn’t. He didn’t know why he hadn’t remembered that.


Wei Jian woke up Ba, and their father drove them to school. Dropped them off at the low, black school gates. The prefect on duty was Wei Jian’s friend.


Wei Ren left his brother behind, went up to his classroom.


Izzat was there first, today.


His friend ran a hand through his curly, unruly hair, and then banged the same hand on the desk. Izzat was loud like that. He said, in Mandarin, “I’m dead. I’m really dead. I hate languages.”


Wei Ren put his bag down by his desk. Looked at Izzat.


“You’re still not talking today?”


Wei Ren thought of the emptiness in his mouth. The silence ringing inside his head. Something cold, something hard, something slick and slimy, it crawled deep inside his skin. But he couldn’t speak.


When they went down for recess, Izzat bought a few sticks of chocolate from the canteen and a bowl of curry noodles. They went to eat at the bus stop across the field from the canteen. In twenty minutes, the prefects on duty would come and chase them away, make sure that Izzat put his empty bowl into one of the large round tubs beside the canteen. But they had twenty minutes.


Izzat looked at him. “You’re not eating today either. Aren’t you hungry? We still have the writing exam after this, you know.”


Wei Ren was sitting on the blue railing, feet braced on the horizontal bar an inch up from the ground. He looked at Izzat.


Izzat was sitting cross-legged on the floor. He set his bowl down. By a crack in the cement. An ant crawled out of the crack. Izzat picked the bowl up again. Waved his chopsticks at Wei Ren. “What’s wrong with you?”


Wei Ren hunched his shoulders. Small, smaller. The exam this morning had been easier than he thought it would be.


He looked down at his left hand. The two missing fingers.


Izzat followed his gaze. He said, “You’ve always been like that. It’s not like anyone cares.” And then, he tilted his curly black head up to the high, high ceiling of the bus stop. “I heard a story, actually. About little fingers. Apparently lovers in Japan used to cut them off and give them to each other. You know, as some kind of promise.” He stuck out his tongue, shivered all over. Izzat was dramatic like that. “Eck, right?”


‘Eck’ wasn’t a sound that people wrote in essays, Wei Ren thought. How did you translate ‘Eck’ into a sound represented by a Chinese character?


“But,” Izzat said, “Intan was all like ‘Eck’ when she heard the story too.” He grinned at Wei Ren, teeth sharp. Izzat didn’t like his older sister, who was in the same year as Wei Ren’s brother and very clever. His voice became flat, the sound of someone else’s words, “People don’t only have one important relationship – romantic love is a cultural construct – or something like that.”


The sun was hot. Wei Ren was beginning to sweat. He used his right hand to pull open the second button on his school shirt.


Footsteps echoed on the cement. Wei Jian, moving up the large steps towards the bus stop. Students crowded those steps, when the primary school had their races across the small field. Wei Jian, walking up them alone, looked smaller than Wei Ren had ever thought that his older brother had looked before.


Wei Jian stopped in front of them. “Five more minutes. Put that bowl where it’s supposed to go.”


“Yes, boss,” Izzat said.


Wei Ren only looked at his brother.


Wei Jian’s hands curled into fists at his sides. He turned sharply away. To the right, straight ahead, then down the steeper, smaller flight of stairs into the long corridor running alongside the field. Some of the older Form 5 boys were eating there. They yelled mocking things as Wei Jian descended the steps.




Wei Ren woke up on a Wednesday morning, and his father was gone.


He knew there was something wrong the moment he opened his eyes. The air in his bedroom was suffocating; he accidentally knocked his alarm clock onto the floor; and Wei Jian didn’t knock on his door, but opened it.


His brother’s face was cast in shadows. Only his eyes were visible, lit by the electric lights in the upstairs hallway. “Wei Ren,” he said. There was something unnaturally quiet in his voice. “We have to walk to school today. We’re going to be late. You need to hurry up.”


As they left the house, walked up the driveway past Ma’s grey Nissan and Ba’s black Toyota, past Wei Ling’s bicycle that she never rode, Wei Ren folded his arms against the early morning cold.


Wei Jian said, “Your hand.”


Wei Ren’s shoes were moving forward, step by step, on the gravel of the small street in their neighbourhood. It was quiet, quiet, quiet, and cold around them. He lifted his left hand, showed it to his brother.


“Your middle finger’s gone,” Wei Jian said.


Wei Ren nodded. He would speak, but his mouth was empty.


Wei Jian’s shoulders hunched, thin in his prefect shirt. He still wasn’t wearing his tie. Hadn’t he gotten in trouble for it by now?


Wei Jian said, “Ren, do you remember anything about last Friday?”


What about last weekend? Wei Ren kept his feet moving forward, round the bend, down by a high garden wall, and thought back.


They had gone to the park last Friday after school. Ma had given them permission. The park was beside the secondary school; there was an ice-cream man there who sold Ding Ding Tong, unlike the ice-cream man in front of the school where Ma came to pick them up. So their mother had said, they could go and buy the Ding Ding Tong, as a pre-exam treat, and she would pick them up at the park gates instead once she had dropped Wei Ling off for the afternoon session.


The park had been empty. Only a little Chinese girl and a little Indian boy, sitting on the swings. They weren’t speaking to each other.


As Wei Jian and Wei Ren walked down the path past the swings, the girl had jumped off, and called out to them. Pointed at Wei Jian’s neck.


“That’s a nice tie, Ko Ko,” she said, politely. “Are you a prefect?”


Wei Jian was nice to all kids except for his own sister and brother. He smiled, with the straight teeth that had resulted from years of wearing braces. “Yes, I am. At that school behind the wall.”


“Can I have it?”


A pause. Wei Jian’s face froze a little, and then he said, “That’s difficult. Ah, it’s the school’s tie.”


The little girl ignored him. She looked at Wei Ren. “He doesn't have a tie.”


“I’m not a prefect,” Wei Ren said, already bored. He put his hands in the pockets of his school pants.


The little Indian boy hadn’t moved from the swing. He was looking over the wall, towards the sloping tiled roof of the school. It was echoing with a rhythmic swell of voices. The afternoon-session primary-school students, chanting the Sanzijing. They sounded bored.


It was hot; the sun beat down on their heads. Wei Ren kicked at a stone on the path. His shadow moved. His brother’s shoes shifted; that shadow moved too.


The little girl had no shadow. She said, speaking to Wei Ren this time, her voice absolutely ordinary, “It’s bad that you don’t have a tie. Living in your house.”


“What do you mean?” Wei Jian said.


The litte Indian boy got up off the swing. Came over to them. He pulled the little girl’s wrist. “We have to go.”


The little girl smiled. Said to Wei Jian and Wei Ren, “You should look where people don’t look.”


The neighbourhood was quiet. The morning sky was beginning to lighten. Wei Ren and Wei Jian would both be late for their first exam.


Wei Ren looked at his brother.


Wei Jian shifted his sling bag from his left to his right shoulder.


At recess that day, Izzat was agitated. He said, in Malay, “You’re still not talking, or eating. And you were late today. What the fuck is wrong with you?”


Wei Ren couldn’t speak. His mouth was empty.


His friend pulled a pen and paper out of his shirt pocket, shoved them in Wei Ren’s face. “Here. I was going to give these to you this morning. But.”


Ah, Wei Ren thought, looking down at the pen and paper. Why hadn’t he thought of this before?


His right hand wouldn’t move.


Izzat dropped his hands to his sides. They were standing opposite each other, in the bus stop. Their shadows ran parallel, disappearing into the deeper shade of the nearby pillar. “What is wrong with you?”


They had been friends since Form One. Izzat had said yesterday that Wei Ren’s hand had always been missing fingers.


Wei Ren shoved his left wrist in Izzat’s face. Waggled the two remaining fingers less than an inch from Izzat’s brown eyes.


Izzat stumbled back.


His gaze was bright with incomprehension.


Wei Ren opened his mouth.


Izzat breathed in, sharply. And then he said, tone blank, “Oh. I forgot. You don’t have a tongue.”


Cold, hard, slick, slimy. Wei Ren dropped his hand.


Izzat laughed. He sounded a little dizzy. “I’m sorry. I forgot. How did I forget?”


The prefect who came to the bus stop this time was Intan.


Wei Ren took the paper, wrote down a question, and held it up to her.


Intan smiled – she was nice, even thought Izzat didn’t like her – and said, slightly puzzled, “Your brother’s group is patrolling the Old Building today. Do you have a sore throat, Wei Ren?”


The Old Building. Wei Ren’s classroom. Wei Ren’s desk. There had been no eraser dust to sweep off this morning, or the morning before that.




Wei Ren woke up on a Thursday morning, and his brother wasn’t gone.


He opened his eyes. The air in his bedroom was thick and dark; he picked up his alarm clock and stashed it under his pillow, listened to it ring. Wei Jian opened his bedroom door. “Turn that off. It’s annoying. Come on, we’re not going to school.”


Then, Wei Ren wanted to ask, why am I waking up?


Wei Jian made breakfast for himself. Not for Wei Ren. Just toast. Butter. He put butter on it in the dining room, knife gleaming in the fluorescent light. It was still dark outside the windows.


They were going to miss their exams.


Wei Jian reached into the pocket of his old, frayed house shorts, and pulled out his prefect’s tie.


Wei Ren sat back in his hard wooden chair.


Had Wei Jian found it? Had he lost it?


Wei Ren had gone straight to sleep again the night before. Instead of studying. He had been very, very tired.


Wei Jian ducked his head. “Ren, do you know where it is that people don’t look?”


Wei Ren opened his mouth. Closed it.


His brother smiled. “That’s right, you can’t talk.”


Why was Wei Jian different? Why did he know that Wei Ren’s fingers had disappeared, instead of having never been there in the first place? Why did he know the same thing about Wei Ren’s tongue?


Wei Jian’s fingers tightened around the blue fabric of his tie. Crumpling it. “This is my primary school tie. My secondary school tie has been missing since Friday night.”


Wei Ren was afraid.


The house was quiet. Very quiet. Wei Ling, Ma, and Ba were gone.


The tie in his brother’s hand was thin, worn. It had been given to Wei Jian, Wei Ren remembered. Only the head prefects were allowed to keep their ties.


“Wei Ren,” his brother said, “Look out of the corner of your eye.”


A beat. A second. A third.


Wei Ren felt frozen in his seat. But, no, his toes shifted, restlessly, cold on the ceramic-tiled floor.


The sky outside the windows was dark.


Wei Ren let his eyes move. His brother’s tie, his brother’s face, the wall of the living room, the corner of the wall, the computer table to Wei Ren’s direct right. And then, the corner of his eye.


Where the archway should be, the open archway to the living room, there was a closed door.


Wei Jian said, quietly, “Don’t move.”


The door creaked, banged. It was loud. Very loud. How had Wei Ren not heard it before now?


Wei Jian reached across the table, took Wei Ren’s arm. His left arm. The grip was firm, the way it had been when they were in primary school, and Ma made Wei Jian hold Wei Ren’s hand as they crossed the road.


It was also cold, the palm clammy, the way that it had been at Gong Gong’s funeral last year, when they were walking down the road with the funeral procession, and someone had shouted. Wei Jian.


Wei Ren had looked up, bored with the proceedings, and seen the headlights first. The windscreen. The face of the driver, white.


Wei Jian pulled him out of the way. The procession scattered. Ma was screaming at them. At the back of the car. At Wei Ling, who had been further back in the group of people moving down the street.


The car had come from around a bend.


To have seen it, Wei Jian had to have been looking out of the corner of his eye.


The door that had never been there before; it creaked, it banged, it groaned.


Wei Jian said, quietly, “Stand up.”


They walked out of the house. Walked until the end of the street, the garden wall.


Wei Ren said, “Did you always know it was there?”


“No,” Wei Jian said. He crouched down on the pavement. Picked up a stone. “What that girl said, it just made me think. Where do people not look? And I remembered – the procession – I was bored. And I looked.”


Wei Ren lifted his hand. The sky was beginning to lighten. He had two remaining fingers. He opened his mouth. It wasn’t empty anymore.


“You can talk,” Wei Jian observed. His voice was thin, strained.


Wei Ren felt something expand in his chest. Then contract. His fingers were still missing. The house was empty. Except for the door.


“We can’t go back,” Wei Jian said. “Not right now.”


Izzat was probably doing their Chemistry exam right at this moment.


Wei Ren curled his left hand into a fist. “What are we going to do?”


Wei Jian put his primary school tie back into his pocket. Had he been carrying it everywhere, all this time?


They were alone, on this quiet street.


A beat. A breath. Wei Jian said, “Everything will be fine.”


Wei Ren lifted his eyes, to his brother’s tense face, the expanse of the garden wall to their right, the flower pot at the edge of the wall. The corner of his eye.


He was afraid.


Because Wei Jian was wrong. The little girl, with no shadow, with the absolutely ordinary voice – she had lied to them. She had been playing with them.


They had lived in this house for ten years. And no one had disappeared.


Ba went to work. Ba had ties too. Many of them. And Ba had disappeared.


Out of the corner of Wei Ren’s eye, the door was opening. With a creak, with a bang, with a shadow and no person to whom the shadow could belong. It slithered out into the early morning light, onto the pavement at Wei Ren’s feet.


Why had his brother looked at him, and known that Wei Ren’s finger was missing? That Monday morning, when Ma cooked them noodles because they had exams.


A game, a shadow, the car driving towards the funeral procession. Wei Jian had pulled him out of the way.


The shadow; it joined Wei Ren’s.


The street was unnaturally empty.


Wei Ren looked down at his hand, still curled into a fist. He imagined another street, another bend, a little Chinese girl and a little Tamil boy sitting on the pavement outside their houses and eating ice-cream. The girl looked up, saw the headlights, the windscreen, the driver’s face.


Who had that driver been? Had he wanted to kill someone?


What did the little girl want from Wei Ren? From his brother?


The darkness expanded beyond his feet, crawled towards Wei Jian, who was fumbling in his pocket for his phone. Maybe to call their aunt, who lived across town. Maybe to call the police.


Cold, hard, slick, and slimy. Wei Ren felt his brain lock up. If his fingers didn’t correspond in the order that he thought they did – if he didn’t do this properly – if he hesitated, stopped, paused –


He inhaled, closed his right hand over his left. Took his forefinger.


He had no knife. This would have to be enough.


He yanked the finger up, in an unnatural direction, as hard as he could. Pulled, and pulled, and pulled. He bit his returned tongue hard enough to bleed.


Bone broke.


Wei Ren hadn’t known he could make that kind of sound before.


His brother dropped his phone.


Wei Ren’s blurring eyes followed the phone, then moved to the right, along the garden wall, the flower pot. The corner of his eye.


The shadow burst. The door closed. There was blood on the pavement.




Wei Ren woke up on a Friday morning.


His left hand was encased in bandages.
